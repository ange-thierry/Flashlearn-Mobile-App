-- CELL BIOLOGY --

Photosynthesis is the process by which plants convert sunlight into chemical energy stored as glucose.
The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell responsible for producing ATP through cellular respiration.
Osmosis refers to the movement of water molecules across a semi-permeable membrane from an area of low solute concentration to high solute concentration.
Cellular respiration is the process by which cells break down glucose to release energy in the form of ATP.
The nucleus is the control centre of the cell containing the genetic material in the form of DNA.
DNA consists of four nucleotide bases: adenine, thymine, guanine, and cytosine.
The cell membrane is made up of a phospholipid bilayer that controls what enters and exits the cell.
Ribosomes are the organelles responsible for synthesising proteins from messenger RNA instructions.
Diffusion refers to the movement of particles from an area of high concentration to an area of low concentration.
The endoplasmic reticulum is the organelle that involves the transport and modification of proteins and lipids within the cell.
An example of an organelle found only in plant cells is the chloroplast.
An example of passive transport is simple diffusion across the cell membrane.

-- ECOLOGY --

An ecosystem consists of all the living organisms and non-living environmental factors interacting in a particular area.
Photosynthesis involves the absorption of carbon dioxide and water to produce glucose and oxygen.
A food chain describes the transfer of energy from one organism to another.
Biodiversity refers to the variety of living species found within a given habitat or region.
The nitrogen cycle is the process by which nitrogen is converted between its various chemical forms as it circulates through the atmosphere and ecosystems.
Primary producers are organisms that are capable of producing their own food through photosynthesis.
An example of a primary consumer is a rabbit feeding on grass in a meadow ecosystem.
Decomposers are organisms that break down dead organic matter, returning nutrients to the soil.
A habitat is the natural environment in which an organism lives and obtains its resources.

-- HUMAN BIOLOGY --

The circulatory system consists of the heart, blood vessels, and blood working together to transport oxygen and nutrients.
Haemoglobin is the protein found in red blood cells responsible for carrying oxygen from the lungs to body tissues.
The nervous system refers to the network of nerves and cells that carry signals between the brain and other parts of the body.
Homeostasis is the process by which the body maintains a stable internal environment despite changes in external conditions.
Enzymes are biological catalysts that are made up of protein molecules and speed up chemical reactions without being consumed.
The respiratory system involves the intake of oxygen and the release of carbon dioxide through the lungs.
Insulin is the hormone produced by the pancreas that regulates blood glucose levels by enabling cells to absorb glucose.
Neurons are specialised cells that are responsible for transmitting electrical signals throughout the nervous system.
An example of a reflex action is the withdrawal of a hand from a hot surface.
The skeletal system consists of bones, cartilage, and ligaments that provide structure and protect internal organs.

-- CHEMISTRY --

An atom consists of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, surrounded by electrons in energy levels.
Ionic bonding refers to the electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions formed by the transfer of electrons.
An element is a pure substance that is made up of only one type of atom and cannot be broken down further by chemical means.
Covalent bonding involves the sharing of electrons between two non-metal atoms to achieve a stable electron configuration.
The periodic table is the organised arrangement of elements in order of increasing atomic number.
A chemical reaction refers to the process by which reactants are transformed into products through the breaking and forming of chemical bonds.
An example of an exothermic reaction is the combustion of methane gas in the presence of oxygen.
Oxidation refers to the loss of electrons or an increase in oxidation state during a chemical reaction.
Reduction refers to the gain of electrons or a decrease in oxidation state during a chemical reaction.
Acids are substances that are capable of donating protons in an aqueous solution and have a pH below 7.

-- PHYSICS --

Newton's first law of motion refers to the tendency of an object to remain at rest or continue in uniform motion unless acted upon by an external force.
Velocity is the speed of an object in a specific direction and is measured in metres per second.
Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses due to its motion.
The speed formula is distance divided by time.
Gravity is the force of attraction between two objects that is proportional to their masses.
Electromagnetic waves are waves that consist of oscillating electric and magnetic fields that travel at the speed of light.
An example of electromagnetic radiation is visible light, which has wavelengths between 400 and 700 nanometres.
Refraction refers to the bending of light as it passes from one medium to another due to a change in speed.
The law of conservation of energy refers to the principle that energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transferred or transformed.
Resistance is the opposition to the flow of electric current in a conductor.

-- HISTORY OF SCIENCE --

Penicillin was discovered by Alexander Fleming in 1928 when he observed mould killing bacteria in a petri dish.
The theory of evolution was introduced by Charles Darwin in his 1859 publication On the Origin of Species.
The structure of DNA was discovered by Watson and Crick in 1953 using X-ray crystallography data.
Gravity as a universal force was established by Isaac Newton in the late seventeenth century.
The periodic table was created by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869 to organise the known elements by atomic mass.
Vaccination was invented by Edward Jenner in 1796 following his work with cowpox and smallpox.
The telephone was invented by Alexander Graham Bell in 1876 following years of experiments with sound transmission.
The theory of relativity was introduced by Albert Einstein in 1905 describing the relationship between space, time, and energy.
